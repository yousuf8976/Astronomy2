The Closest Planet to the Sun

It is the closest planet to the Sun and the smallest in our solar system. Discover its extreme temperatures and unique features.

Mercury

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and the smallest in our solar system. Due to its proximity to the Sun, it has an extremely high surface temperature during the day, reaching up to 800°F (427°C). However, its thin atmosphere can't retain heat, causing temperatures to plummet to -330°F (-201°C) at night. Mercury has no moons and no significant atmosphere to speak of, which makes its surface susceptible to extreme conditions, such as solar radiation and meteor impacts. Despite its small size, it has a dense core made mostly of iron. The planet’s orbital period is only 88 Earth days, making its year much shorter than ours.

Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system and the closest to the Sun. With a diameter of just 3,032 miles (4,880 km), it is only slightly larger than Earth's Moon. Despite its small size, Mercury has a dense, iron-rich core that makes up about 75% of the planet's volume. This gives it a strong gravitational pull, about 38% that of Earth's. The planet has no atmosphere to speak of, which leads to extreme temperature fluctuations. During the day, temperatures can soar to 800°F (427°C), while at night, they can drop to a bone-chilling -330°F (-201°C).

These drastic temperature changes are due to the lack of an insulating atmosphere. Mercury has no moons, and its surface is dotted with craters and basins, much like the Moon’s surface. The planet has a very slow rotation, taking 59 Earth days to complete a full spin, but it only takes 88 Earth days to orbit the Sun. The planet's day-night cycle is complex, and its surface shows signs of past volcanic activity, though there is no current volcanic activity.

Because of its proximity to the Sun, Mercury experiences intense solar radiation, which makes its surface conditions even more extreme. Scientists believe Mercury may have once had a thicker atmosphere that was stripped away by solar winds. The lack of atmosphere also means that Mercury has no weather patterns, making it a stark, barren world with no clouds or precipitation.

Despite these extreme conditions, Mercury is rich in minerals, and some scientists believe it may contain vast deposits of water ice in the permanently shadowed regions at its poles. These ice deposits are believed to have been preserved for billions of years. Mercury's surface is covered in deep, craggy cliffs, called "lobate scarps," which stretch for hundreds of miles. These scarps were likely caused by the planet cooling and shrinking over time.

The absence of an atmosphere also means that Mercury has no protection from meteors, which frequently impact its surface, creating craters. NASA's MESSENGER mission, which orbited Mercury from 2011 to 2015, provided invaluable data on the planet's surface and composition.
