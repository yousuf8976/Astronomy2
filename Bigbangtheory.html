The Big Bang Theory

Explore the origins of the universe and the explosive beginning of everything.

What is the Big Bang Theory?

The Big Bang Theory is the leading explanation of how the universe began. It suggests that the universe was once in an extremely hot, dense, and compact state. Around 13.8 billion years ago, it began expanding and has continued to do so ever since.

According to this theory, all matter and energy were condensed into a singularity, an infinitely small point. Then, in a fraction of a second, it began expanding rapidly. This event, known as the "Big Bang," marked the beginning of the universe as we know it.

After the Big Bang, the universe began to cool, leading to the formation of subatomic particles and, later, atoms. Over billions of years, these atoms began to clump together, forming galaxies, stars, and eventually planets, including our own Earth.

The evidence supporting the Big Bang Theory includes the observation of cosmic microwave background radiation, the abundance of light elements in the universe, and the redshift of distant galaxies, all of which suggest the universe is still expanding.

The Big Bang is not an explosion in space; instead, it is the expansion of space itself. The theory also explains how the universe has evolved over time, from its hot, dense beginning to the present day, and how it might continue to evolve in the future.

While much has been discovered, there is still much to learn. Scientists continue to study the cosmic background radiation and explore dark matter and dark energy, which make up the majority of the universe's mass-energy content and remain mysterious.
