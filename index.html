Mercury

Description: Mercury is the smallest and closest planet to the Sun, with extreme temperature variations and a heavily cratered surface.

Diameter: 4,880 km.

Venus

Description: Venus is Earth's twin in size but has a thick, toxic atmosphere and extreme surface temperatures due to a runaway greenhouse effect.

Diameter: 12,104 km.

Earth

Description: Earth is the only planet known to support life, with a diverse climate, abundant water, and a protective atmosphere.

Diameter: 12,742 km.

Mars

Description: Mars, known as the Red Planet, has iron oxide-rich soil and is a prime target for exploration due to its history of water.

Diameter: 6,779 km.

Jupiter

Description: Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system, a gas giant with a strong magnetic field and a Great Red Spot.

Diameter: 139,820 km.



Saturn

Description: Saturn is known for its stunning ring system and is a gas giant composed mostly of hydrogen and helium.

Diameter: 116,460 km.

Uranus

Description: Uranus is an ice giant with a distinctive blue color, rotating on its side and having a faint ring system.

Diameter: 50,724 km.

Neptune

Description: Neptune is the farthest planet from the Sun, an ice giant with intense storms and a deep blue color from methane.

Diameter: 49,244 km.

THE BIG BANG THEORY

The Big Bang Theory is the leading explanation for the origin of the universe, proposing that it began around 13.8 billion years ago from an incredibly hot and dense state. This monumental explosion initiated the expansion of space, leading to the formation of galaxies, stars, and eventually planets. Key evidence supporting this theory includes the observable redshift of distant galaxies and the Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMB), which is the afterglow of the initial explosion. Over time, the universe cooled, allowing for the creation of light elements like hydrogen and helium. The Big Bang Theory not only explains how the universe began but also provides insights into its ongoing expansion and evolution.

CONSTELLATIONS

Constellations are groups of stars that form recognizable patterns in the night sky, often named after mythological figures, animals, or objects. These stellar groupings, while appearing close together from Earth, are actually spread out over vast distances across space. Ancient civilizations used constellations for navigation, calendar keeping, and storytelling, embedding them with cultural and historical significance. Today, astronomers continue to use these patterns to map the sky and locate celestial objects. The 88 officially recognized constellations cover the entire celestial sphere, helping us divide the sky into manageable sections for scientific observation. Despite their beauty, constellations are not physical structures but optical illusions created by our perspective from Earth.
